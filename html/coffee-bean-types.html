1. Arabica

Coffea arabica is the most widely consumed coffee species in the world, known for its complex aroma and balanced flavor. It is believed to have originated in Ethiopia and later spread through Yemen, where coffee began to be cultivated as an agricultural crop. As coffee trading expanded, Arabica plants were introduced to regions across Asia, Africa, and the Americas, leading to the development of many different varieties.

Arabica coffee is typically grown at higher elevations, usually between 800 and 2,000 meters above sea level, in climates with average temperatures around 20°C. Compared to other coffee species, it generally contains less caffeine and is valued for its floral, fruity, and nuanced flavor profile. For this reason, Arabica beans are commonly used in specialty coffee and single-origin coffees.

Most Arabica varieties can be traced back to two historically important lineages: Typica and Bourbon. These varieties played a key role in the global spread of coffee and became the genetic foundation for many modern Arabica cultivars.

Health Benefits of Arabica

Arabica coffee is rich in antioxidants, helping to combat oxidative stress and reduce inflammation. Its lower caffeine content provides a steady boost to mental clarity and alertness without the intense jitteriness often linked with higher caffeine intake. Furthermore, the meticulous cultivation process required for Arabica ensures high-quality beans rich in beneficial compounds that support a healthy lifestyle.

Varieties of Arabica:

Typica Origin: Ethiopia

The ancestral variety of all Arabica coffees. It has a relatively low yield per unit area compared to other varieites, and is susceptible to pests and diseases, but is highly prized for its excellent flavor and clean, bright acidity.

  • Representative Beans: Jamaica Blue Mountain, Hawaiian Kona
  • Characteristics: Elongated bean shape, clear and clean sweetness, delicate flavor profile
Bourbon Origin: Yemen / Réunion

A variety closely related to the original Arabica. It originated in Yemen and later naturally mutated on Réunion Island (formerly called Bourbon Island). In the 18th century, French missionaries helped spread it widely. Bourbon was introduced to Brazil around 1860, where it interbred with other regional Bourbon varieties from Ethiopia and India. Its yield is higher than Typica, but still lower than many modern varieties. Coffee cherries come in various colors, including red, yellow, and orange. Bourbon is known for its full body and complex, wine-like acidity.

  • Representative Beans: Yellow Bourbon, Red Bourbon
  • Characteristics: Rounded bean shape, excellent balance and well-rounded flavor
Mundonovo Origin: Brazil

A natural hybrid of Bourbon and Typica, discovered in Brazil in 1943. Distribution to Brazilian farms began in 1952. The name “Mundo Novo” means “New World” in Portuguese, reflecting the hope for discovering a promising new variety at the time. Mundo Novo has high yields, can be cultivated at altitudes of 1,000-1,200 meters, and is relatively resistant to pests and diseases.

  • Characteristics: Rounded bean shape, ordinary flavor, low-toned acidity, excellent sweetness, full-bodied profile
  • Note: It is widely used as a base for espresso blends and was later crossed with Caturra to create the popular 'Catuai' variety.
Caturra Origin: Minas Gerais, Brazil

A natural mutation of the Bourbon variety, discovered on a farm in Minas Gerais, Brazil—a major state responsible for producing over 50% of all Brazilian coffee. Today, it is widely cultivated throughout Central and South America. Caturra is a dwarf variety with a significantly shorter stature than its ancestors, making it much easier for farmers to hand-pick the cherries. It also allows for denser planting, resulting in a high yield per cultivation area.

  • Characteristics: The beans are relatively small and dense. The plant produces both red and yellow coffee cherries (Red Caturra and Yellow Caturra). In the cup, it generally offers a bright, vibrant acidity with a light-to-medium body.
Maragogype Origin: Brazil

A natural mutation of Typica discovered in Brazil. Maragogype is famous for its remarkably large bean size, earning it the nickname "Elephant Bean." The beans are nearly twice the size of average coffee varieties.

  • Characteristics: Exceptionally large beans and cherries, smooth flavor, and a light-to-medium body.
Catuai Origin: Brazil

A hybrid variety created by crossing Caturra and Mundo Novo. The name "Catuai" means "Very Good" in an indigenous Brazilian language (Tupi-Guarani). It combines the dwarf stature of Caturra with the vigor of Mundo Novo, making it highly resistant to strong winds and rain. While highly productive, the tree has a shorter lifespan (about 10 years shorter) than other varieties.

  • Characteristics: High yield, resilience to harsh weather, and a clean acidity with a sweet finish.
Villa Sarchi Origin: Sarchi, Costa Rica

A natural mutation of Bourbon with a single-gene mutation, discovered in the village of Sarchi, Costa Rica, in the 1960s. The beans are smaller than standard Bourbon. It is known for its high productivity and excellent cup quality, thriving even in high altitudes and strong wind conditions.

  • Characteristics: Elegant acidity, medium body, and excellent sweetness.
Pacas Origin: Santa Ana, El Salvador

A natural mutation of Bourbon discovered in the Santa Ana region of El Salvador in 1949. Similar to other Bourbon mutations, it has a compact (dwarf) size. It is highly regarded for its bright acidity and consistent body, inheriting the best qualities of the Bourbon lineage.

  • Characteristics: Small tree size, vibrant acidity, and good body.
Pacamara Origin: El Salvador

A hybrid variety created by crossing Pacas and Maragogype—its name is a combination of the first four letters of each parent. It inherits the large bean and cherry size from Maragogype and the high productivity from Pacas.

  • Characteristics: Large bean size, complex flavor profile ranging from floral and fruity to herbal notes, and a creamy body.
Geisha Origin: Ethiopia (Wild discovery)

First discovered in the wild forests of Ethiopia in the 1930s, Geisha was initially overlooked due to its difficult cultivation process. However, its fate changed forever in 2005 when it appeared at a Best of Panama auction from the Hacienda La Esmeralda farm. It received an unprecedented score of 98 points, selling at a price nearly 100 times higher than commercial coffee at the time. Since then, Geisha has become one of the most prestigious and sought-after varieties in the specialty coffee world.

  • Flavor Profile: Celebrated for its extraordinary floral aroma (like jasmine), vibrant citrus acidity, and sweet notes of honey.
  • Characteristics: It has a distinctively long and slender bean shape. Because it is highly sensitive to its environment, it requires very specific high-altitude conditions to develop its full flavor potential.

2. Robusta

Coffee canephora (commonly known as Robusta) is one of the two primary coffee species consumed worldwide, accounting for approximately 40% of global coffee production. It is native to central and western Africa and has been widely cultivated across tropical regions.

Robusta coffee plants are hardy and adaptable, capable of growing in a range of climates and at lower elevations. They are more resistant to pests and diseases, partly due to their higher caffeine content, which also reduces the need for chemical pesticides. These characteristics have led to increasing interest in Robusta as a more sustainable coffee crop.

Compared to Arabica, Robusta contains more caffeine and is known for its stronger, more bitter flavor profile, often described as earthy or nutty. It is commonly used in espresso blends to enhance body and crema, as well as in instant coffee production.

Health Benefits of Robusta

Robusta is praised for its high caffeine levels, which provide a substantial energy boost and may increase metabolic rates, aiding in weight management. It contains significant amounts of chlorogenic acids, powerful antioxidants that help fight free radicals and reduce inflammation. Accounting for about 30% of global production, it serves as a vital and accessible caffeine source worldwide.

3. Liberica

Coffee liberica is another species of coffee plant, alongside Arabica and Robusta. It represents a small portion of global coffee production, accounting for approximately 1-2%, and is primarily cultivated in Southeast Asia, particularly in the Philippines, Malaysia, and Indonesia.

Liberica originated in West Africa, specifically Liberia, and was introduced to Southeast Asia in the late 19th century during a widespread outbreak of coffee leaf rust. This disease severely affected Arabica and, to a lesser extent, Robusta, leading many farmers to adopt Liberica due to its relatively strong resistance.

Although Liberica is not entirely immune to coffee leaf rust, its cultivation persisted in regions where it proved more adaptable. Today, it remains an important coffee species in certain countries, where it constitutes a significant share of local production.

Compared to other coffee species, Liberica is known for its distinctive flavor profile, often described as bold, woody, and sometimes smoky, with a full body and unique aromatic qualities.
